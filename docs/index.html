I.
Why trees?

Non-linear interactions, for free.

A logistic regression looks for one straight boundary. A decision tree can carve the space into rectangles — "tenure < 12 AND returns_per_purchase > 0.10" is one split, no feature engineering required. And no scaler in sight.

Logistic regression · one line

The straight boundary.

Linear models draw one hyperplane. When churners cluster in two opposite corners, a single line cannot separate them — accuracy hits a ceiling unless you hand-engineer an interaction term.

Decision tree · recursive rectangles

The step boundary.

if tenure < 0.4 & returns > 0.55 → churn
elif tenure > 0.6 & returns > 0.70 → churn
elif tenure < 0.15 & returns < 0.25 → churn
else → stay

Trees split on thresholds, not distances — so they pick up interactions automatically and ignore scale. Skip StandardScaler for tree models. You still need to one-hot encode categoricals.

II.
Two philosophies

Bagging averages. Boosting corrects.

Random forests and gradient boosters both ensemble many trees, but they reach the answer from opposite directions. One reduces variance by averaging independent trees. The other builds expertise one mistake at a time.

Random forest · bagging.

Grow many deep trees, each on a different bootstrap sample and a random subset of features. Average their votes.

  • Trains in parallel — trees are independent.
  • Variance ↓, bias unchanged — smooths out the wild guesses of a single deep tree.
  • Robust default — sklearn-only, sane defaults, stable across retrains.
  • Feature importance is easy to compute and easy to explain.

Gradient boosting · residual fitting.

Start with a weak prediction. Fit a small tree to its errors. Add it. Repeat — each new tree corrects the running mistakes of the ensemble.

  • Trains sequentially — tree N+1 depends on tree N.
  • Higher accuracy ceiling — usually beats forests on tabular data, by a little.
  • Sensitive to learning_rate, n_estimators, depth. Use early stopping.
  • Brittle across retrains — the same code on next week's data may not reproduce.

V.
A checklist

Three checks before you ship.

After tuning, you'll often have two or three candidates within a few F1 points of each other. Skip these and you'll ship the model that won on a metric, not the one the business can operate.

1

Is the gap real?

Compare the F1 difference against the cross-validation standard deviation. If the gap sits inside that noise, treat the models as tied and let operations break the tie.

✕ "Booster 0.753, Forest 0.731. Ship booster." ✓ "Δ0.022 vs sd ≈ 0.018. Inside noise."
2

Who maintains it?

Sklearn-only beats adding a new dependency for tied accuracy. Random forest is more stable across retrains; boosting can be brittle. The model someone else can debug at 2am wins ties.

✕ "+ xgboost, + lightgbm, + optuna." ✓ "Sklearn. No new infra to learn."
3

Who questions it?

If a regulator, executive, or customer might ask why?, a forest's feature-importance story beats a booster's. Match the model to the audience that will challenge it.

✕ "Most accurate — trust the math." ✓ "Feature importance: tenure, returns, tickets."

VI.
Self-check

Eight questions from the field notebook.

Try each mentally first. Click to flip the card. Drawn from this week's takeaways — the notebooks have the worked examples.

▸ Click to reveal Do tree models need StandardScaler?
No. Trees split on thresholds, not distances — a feature in pounds and one in proportions get the same chance. You still need to one-hot encode categoricals; you do not need to scale. But skip scaling explicitly, don't forget it.
▸ Click to reveal What is the one-sentence difference between bagging and boosting?
Bagging averages many independent deep trees to reduce variance. Boosting grows trees sequentially, each fitting the running ensemble's residuals, to reduce bias. Parallel vs sequential; smoothing vs error-correction.
▸ Click to reveal Why do you lower learning_rate when you raise n_estimators?
Each boosting round nudges the prediction by learning_rate × tree_output. A lower learning rate makes each nudge smaller, so you need more rounds to reach the same fit. The product matters more than either value alone. Use early stopping to auto-pick the count.
▸ Click to reveal You tuned the model for f1. The Friday slide reports accuracy. What's wrong?
You optimised one thing and reported another. The threshold that maximises F1 is not the same one that maximises accuracy; on imbalanced data they can pull in opposite directions. Use the same scoring metric for tuning, validation, and the business decision — or be explicit about why they differ.
▸ Click to reveal Booster F1 = 0.753 ± 0.018. Forest F1 = 0.731 ± 0.012. Is the gap real?
Marginally. The 0.022 difference sits just outside the booster's CV standard deviation — statistically there, but small. Whether you ship the booster depends on whether the gap survives the operational columns (dependency cost, explainability, retrain stability). On a tie, operations decide.
▸ Click to reveal A regulator asks: "Why did the model flag this customer?" Which model is easier to defend?
Random forest. Sklearn's feature_importances_ gives a stable, defensible ranking ("tenure, returns, ticket-rate together drive 60% of decisions"). Gradient boosting's importances are noisier and individual-tree paths are messier. Match the model to the audience that will challenge it.
▸ Click to reveal Why does a deep single decision tree usually overfit?
With no depth limit, the tree keeps splitting until each leaf contains a tiny, peculiar subset of training rows — effectively memorising the data. Training F1 nears 1.0; validation F1 drops. Constrain via max_depth, min_samples_leaf, or ensemble many trees to average the memorisation away (that's bagging).
▸ Click to reveal The booster wins on F1 but adds xgboost as a dependency. What questions do you ask before shipping it?
Three: (1) Is the F1 gap outside the CV standard deviation? (2) Can the team operate the new dependency — install, debug, retrain — without a single point of failure? (3) Will anyone need to explain a flag to a regulator or customer? If any answer is no, the simpler model usually wins.
